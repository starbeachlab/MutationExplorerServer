Frequently Asked Questiones

  • How can I upload structures?
    There are three different ways of uploading: from the Protein Databank (PDB), from the AlphaFold website, from your local machine. All three possibilities are shown on the initial submit page. You have to provide the PDB ID, the AlphaFold ID, or select a file from your computer, accordingly.
  • How can I define mutations?
    Once you submitted the structure, a second page opens. On that page you are asked to define your mutations. There are four ways of defining your mutations:
    1. define any number of mutations manually using the graphical user interface. See FAQ (link to next) for a detailed explanation of the syntax.
    2. upload sequencing data (VCF format).
    3. upload a sequence alignment (clustalW format)
    4. provide a target sequence
      1. upload file (Fasta format)
      2. Uniprot-ID
      3. paste sequence
  • What is the syntax for manually defining mutations?
    For each mutation you have to provide in this order:
    1. the chain as written in the PDB file
    2. the ’:’ separator
    3. the residue number
    4. the one letter code of the target amino acid
    You can define any number of mutations, separated by commas. Example: ”A:12G,B:123W”: two mutations are defined, first, in chain A residue 12 is mutated into glycine, second, in chain B residue 123 is mutated into tryptophan.
  • How is the VCF sequencing format evaluated?
    First, a suitable PDB is determined. Second, the mutations are applied.
  • What coloring schemes are available?
    You can color structures by energy, sequence conservation and properties such as hydropho- bicities. For the energy coloring red means an increased energy due to the mutation(s), blue an increased energy, and white no change. For sequence conservation blue means conserved and red means not conserved. For hydrophobicity blue means hydrophilic and red means hydrophobic.
  • How to use an alignment to define mutations?
    There are two reasons for using alignments. First, an alignment is a way of defining potentially many mutations. Second, by looking at differences in structure and energy can provide deep insights into the alignment itself. Obviously, only aligned positions can be used to define mutations. Gaps cannot be translated into mutations.

  • Is it possible to mutate peptides?
    The smaller the peptide the more likely it is to be flexible. MutationExplorer has no restrictions concerning the size of the protein being uploaded. However, you should be aware that any conformation is just a snapshot and the smaller your peptide the more likely a given conformation may not represent the effect of a mutation on another conformation.
  • Is it possible to mutate transmembrane proteins?
    In general yes. However, there is no energetic effect due to the lipid bilayer considered at this point.
